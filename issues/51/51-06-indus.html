Students Voice Concerns in INDUS forum

yueni zhong

 

About 30 students attended a hate-crime forum hosted by Indus and moderated by professor Kiren Chaudhry. Held on seventh floor Eshleman, the forum provided a space of discussion for a community of students who express a diversity of opinions to demonstrate their solidarity in a common concern, while simultaneously asserting their various different ethnic backgrounds.

Many students shared similar anecdotes about their experiencing distance from white Americans since September 11th, especially students who came from suburban areas where they felt they mixed in pretty well with their white neighbors pre-September 11. Some expressed concerns in discussing international relations and US foreign policy, fearing that they would be considered un-American and risk phycial danger if they do so.

An interesting point of discussion arose when South Asian students express their solidarity with Arab Americans, saying that even though now that South Asian political coalitions are forming to teach more Americans about Sikh Americans, Hindu Americans and Muslim Americans, few try to say “I am not Arab” or “I am not Muslim” Yet others are concerned that these cross-ethnic solidarity to counteract racial lumping are often short-live because they only form when immediate physical danger is involved and are quite ephemeral considering much conflict still exist between various South Asian groups.

On a whole the forum raised more questions on the complexity of issues surrounding September 11th such as : How does 9.11 test our capacity to distinguish between film and reality? Why are Americans, so proud of the belief that someone is innocent before proven guilty, are so eager for revenge when no hard evidence has been presented? How does US foreign policy relate to the occurrence of hate crimes? Does patriotism mean supporting the current government and the mainstream political stance? Or does patriotism mean holding up to the ideals of the US constitution even when that means speaking out against the government? Is Islamic Fundamentalism the real root of the problem? Why are there not more teach-in’s about the socio-economic and the geo-political implications of American presence in the Southwest Asia and North Africa?

Obviously, no one can agree on any given issue despite many of the participants share the same concern for their own safeties. Professor Chaudhry encouraged more critical discussions on the recent events, more teach-ins and suggests holding more future forums that also include Arab Americans.

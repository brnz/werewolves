Sex Trafficking in South and Southeast Asian on the Rise

sameena azhar

 

Globalization means a lot of different things to a lot of different people. It means seeing a McDonald’s in the middle of rural Thailand. It means seeing a child wearing a Mickey Mouse T-shirt in Japan. It means millions of South Africans will receive HIV antiretroviral medication. But for an unknown and increasing number of women in South and Southeast Asia, it means being bought into the sex trade.

On August 20, 2001, UC Berkeley’s Institute of Industrial Trade conducted a symposium entitled, Sex Trafficking in the Global Economy. This round-table discussion focused on trafficking issues particular to South Asia. In attendance were two representatives from each of the five major South Asian nations (Bangladesh, India, Nepal, Pakistan and Sri Lanka). Some representatives included Paul Rodriques, Director, “Under Privileged People’s Development Organization,” Bangladesh; Ishwari Bhattari, Under Secretary, Ministry of Women, Children and Social Welfare, Nepal; Uzma Noorani, Council Member, Human Rights Commission, Pakistan; and Reverend Sister Mary Kathleen, Women’s Desk, “Social and Economic Development Centre,” Sri Lanka. The group, who was sponsored by the US State Department, was in the midst of a three-week nation-wide tour. Their conversation was recorded by National Public Radio.

A diverse collage of individuals, the group included a labor attorney, a heath worker, a therapeutic counselor, a nun and social workers. They discussed several issues affecting the proliferation of sexual trafficking, including globalization, substance abuse, HIV infection and child abuse.

International Efforts to Combat Trafficking

Let us first clarify what the term trafficking means. A 1994 Utrecht Conference resolution stated that, “The traffic in persons is not only for purposes of prostitution, but for a range of other activities as well...It is important to emphasize that the element that defines traffic is force and not the nature of labor to be performed.”

The 1998 US Senate Resolution 82 on Trafficking confirmed, “Trafficking involves one or more forms of kidnapping, false imprisonment, rape, battering, forced labor, or slavery-like practices which violate fundamental human rights.” The resolution emphasizes, “Trafficking consists of all acts involved in the recruitment or transportation of persons within or across borders, involving deception, coercion or force, abuse of authority, debt bondage or fraud, for the purpose of placing persons in situations of abuse or exploitation such as forced prostitution, battering and extreme cruelty, sweatshop labor or exploitative domestic servitude.”

Sexual trafficking is those particular forms of trafficking involving the violation of personal sexuality. This can be manifest in the forms of childhood molestation, childhood pornography, forced prostitution, rape, or coercive sexual practices. The United Nations estimates that 4 million people are trafficked every year, resulting in $7 billion in profits to criminal groups. According to the New York Times, the CIA reports that 45,000 to 50,000 women are imported illegally into the United States every year for the purposes of sex trafficking. Though accurate figures are hard to obtain for this covert activity, it is estimated that 700,000 persons are victims of trafficking worldwide every year. Trafficking in persons has been an expanding criminal enterprise since the mid-1990s. The INS identified at least 250 brothels in the United States this year likely to be using trafficked women.

Current movements to end trafficking had their roots in the 1995 UN Women’s Conference in Beijing, but were given a substantial push of encouragement by American politicians. Last year, Senator Sam Brownback (R, Kansas) led a fact-finding mission to Nepal, where he met with several one-time sex slaves who are now in their teens and dying of AIDS. Brownback and Senator Paul Wellstone (D, Minnesota) became backers of legislation that provides 20-year sentences for criminals who entice victims into sexual slavery. Earlier this year, the Sex Trafficking Victims Protection Act (part of the larger Victims Protection Act of 2000) was also passed. The act provides funding to combat the trafficking of women in the sex trade in the United States and around the world, through prevention education, prosecution of traffickers, and victim assistance. Particularly, the act protects women who were brought into the United States for sexual purposes by providing them asylum. Victims will be given access to translators and will be eligible for housing, medical care and law officer protection pending prosecutions.

Attorney General John Ashcroft commented, “Human trafficking victims often are too young, too frightened, too trapped in their circumstances to speak for themselves. We hope to be the victim’s voice, to lessen the suffering, to prosecute those who commit these crimes to the fullest extent of the law.”

On July 12, 2001, the US State Department released a Congressionally-mandated report on the trade of human beings, reviewing what 82 nations are doing to combat it. Secretary of State Colin Powell commented, “It is incomprehensible that trafficking in persons should be taking place in the 21st century. But it is true, very true.”

Powell said an interagency task force is being formed within the US government to find ways that law enforcement, workplace regulators, border control officials and others can work together “to put an end to this abomination against humanity.”

Action must be taken internationally to put an end to trafficking, according to Under Secretary for Global Affairs Paula Dobriansky. Dobriansky commented, “This report is not a discussion of which country has the greatest widespread trafficking problem, or where the most number of cases of trafficking exist, but rather it is a review of what governments are doing to fight the problem.” 23 countries were found to not be doing enough to combat international trafficking.

State Department officials cited the importance of the UN Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons as another factor motivating nations to take stronger action to stop the problem. The United States and 80 other nations signed the protocol in December of 2000.

Causes and Effects of Sex Trafficking

In the West, we mistakenly view prostitution as always being voluntary. However, in several South and Southeast Asian countries, women are oftentimes deceived into becoming prostitutes. Seeking economic opportunities abroad, women turn to a variety of resources, including newspaper advertisements, acquaintances, marriage agencies, labor recruiters, and modeling agencies. They accept positions (primarily in domestic services) as nannies, maids, factory workers, and hostesses. They believe they will be working in domestic labor in a foreign country for good pay, but then are duped into becoming prostitutes. They may become part of a brothel ring from which they cannot escape or cannot return to home.

Women are most often trafficked from smaller, poorer rural communities to larger, wealthier urban communities, resulting in the creation of networks that recruit and transport women illegally for slavery-like work, including forced prostitution, sweatshop labor, and exploitative domestic servitude. Many migrants end up as victims of such unscrupulous trafficking networks.

Often times, poverty-struck women are forced to make money in any way they can, and thus agree to become sex workers not for the glamour of the sex trade, but in order to get by. Young women, tired of living in rural poverty with no future, find themselves ready and willing to take a chance at a new life promised to them by agents, who tactfully approach the girl’s parents. Many of these young women have already been exposed to the dream of living in luxury as fed to them through television and other mass media. Some imagine that they will receive luxuries and engage in an easy lifestyle. Prostitution seems to them to be the only means available to actualize these dreams. Among teenagers it is also popular to follow the lead of one’s peer group. In a survey conducted in Bangkok, anthropologist Dilok-Udomchai found that 37% of prostitutes joined the sex trade because they went along with a pattern set by their friends.

Negative social values that denigrate women also contribute to the problem of prostitution. Asian societies place a great deal on emphasis on virginity, which is to be preserved for one’s husband until marriage. Yet this overemphasized value has backfired. Among rape cases, young girls think that once they have lost their virginity, they have lost their value. Consequently, they believe their only option is to become a prostitute.

Economic factors are another major cause of prostitution. Girls sometimes become prostitutes out of a sense of duty to their parents or to share the family’s economic burden. The majority of South and Southeast Asian prostitutes come from agricultural backgrounds, from families without economic stability. In some years, their families’ agricultural yield is enough only to meet basic survival needs. Other families work on rented land and have to return half the yield to landowners. What remains is not sufficient to meet expensive costs, like pesticides and farm equipment, some of which are acquired on loan. Some farming families find themselves in substantial debt, far beyond their ability to repay. When the family is in great debt, the eldest daughters are sometimes asked to “sacrifice” for their parents and their younger siblings. There are cases where fathers have sold and re-sold their daughters into prostitution to buy extra cows for farming. This is done in the belief that children must show gratitude to their parents and earn their keep in the household.

Linked with economic instability and poverty is a lack of education. The majority of prostitutes have had only four years of compulsory education and are between ten and nineteen years old. Farmers who have had to struggle to survive economically are not able to provide higher education for their children. Many families of farmers do not have adequate knowledge of family planning. As a result, most have large families, accentuating their economic burden. A high percentage of prostitutes have many siblings, and are themselves the eldest daughters. Economic factors cause further problems: the break up of families; husbands deserting wives and children; and great numbers of rural, unskilled poor who come to seek jobs in big cities, often with little success, as most of them are unskilled. To understand the complexity of sexual trafficking, we must realize that it takes a myriad of forms internationally.

The GI Syndrome and Comfort Women

The Southeast Asian prostitution industry, particularly that of Thailand and the Philippines, escalated dramatically during the 1960’s when the United States established military bases throughout Southeast Asia during the Vietnam War. Several Western countries occupy military bases or harbor major ports throughout Southeast Asia, namely in Manila, Bangkok, Katmandu and Okinawa. Women living around these bases often earn a livelihood by working as exotic dancers, masseuses, hostesses and prostitutes. Often, the GI’s develop a “syndrome” wherein they feel themselves to be “helping” these women by offering them their “services” as a client. Westerners also often foster Orientalist conceptions of Asian women as subservient, racially inferior sex slaves. Thus, Asian sex workers serve as “comfort women,” giving the GI’s a sense of home life, love, and caring while they are abroad.

Sex tourism: One Night in Bangkok

Even after the American military bases were dismantled, prostitution continued to spread in various guises— bar girls, female escorts, singers, exotic dancers, and masseuses. For several parts of Southeast Asia, sex trafficking became a major source of economic tourism. Sex tourism is one of the primary sources of economic stability for Thailand and the Philippines. International customers flock to centers such as Bangkok, Goa and Manila, knowing that they can expect to get cheap sex.

Mail-Order Brides

In recent years, hundreds of websites and catalogues have popped up, catering to a growing clientele of men seeking “mail-order brides.” A chilling description of a Filipina mail-order bride website reads, “On the following pages you will find over 1700 girls from the ages of 18 to 50 who are anxious to meet you. Don’t be afraid to write to a girl 20 years or more years younger than yourself.” Websites include pictures of eligible women, accompanied by their “bio-data,” including their age, height, weight, education, number of children and bust/waist measurements. The listings of women will undoubtedly strike any reader as a Sears catalogue of priced items. On-line marketing strategies include such abominable promotions as “Two for the Price of One,” “Summer Reductions,” and “Add Item to Shopping Cart.” Undoubtedly such terminology reinforces the sexual objectification of women by such businesses.

Lack of Regulation

One might ask why sexual trafficking is so unregulated. The lack of regulation is due to several factors. Some contributing factors include increased economic globalization and negative stereotyping about sex workers. In addition, sex workers face several risks, including venereal diseases like HIV/AIDS, genital herpes, gonorrhea, syphilis and chlammydia; rape and/or sexual assault; domestic violence; and arrest or prosecution. But perhaps the greatest effect of prostitution on women is the devalued self-conception and loss of a sense of independent agency. Mr. Muniraj from “Outreach,” a program which caters to the needs of unwatched children living near construction sites in Bangalore, writes in his organization’s newsletter, “In today’s affluent society, it becomes easy to forget that there are people less fortunate…people who are illiterate, starving, and very often exploited by the members of a harsh and uncaring world.” It is reassuring to know that this dedicated group of activists will not let the phenomenon of sexual trafficking be over-looked by the international community, but will continue to give voice to the concerns of innumerable exploited women and children in South and Southeast Asia.

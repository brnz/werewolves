Monsoon Wedding: New Film Storms Theaters this Season!

jane yin

 

party time: The bride is ready for Monsoon Wedding

“Life is such a comedy. No signal up there, but one down here.”

Exclaims a frustrated P.K. Dube, the wedding planner in the highly anticipated film, “Monsoon Wedding.” His cell phone breaks up mid-conversation while he’s flying atop the wedding tent, scrambling to get the wedding party organized. This sets the picture of the anxious ambiance of the big wedding that is about to occur. “Monsoon Wedding,” a beautiful film directed and produced by critically acclaimed film-maker Mira Nair, depicts a Punjabi family in the midst of preparing for a young woman’s arranged betrothal to an engineer from Houston, Texas. Nair tells a superb tale that encompasses every facet of an Indian wedding and brilliantly delineates the intricacies of this family. The themes and issues that are addressed in the film are universal and produce a touching end. The wedding is not only a union of two strangers but also a union of the family members and friends. This movie paints a beautiful picture of the boundlessness of unconditional love.

Sometimes the most stressful situations can produce the funniest scenarios, as is the case in Monsoon Wedding. As the movie opens up, the funny interactions between the various family members, due to the stress of the fast-coming wedding, introduces us to the close-knit Verma family and their extended family. But as the film progresses, we learn of family secrets, desires, and problems. Each character travels through their path of growth, and in the end, are able to celebrate this momentous occasion together. This film has already won Best Film, Golden Lion at the 2001 Venice International Film Festival, where it debuted. It also received a nomination for Best Foreign Language Film at the 2002 Golden Globes.

Mira Nair began her career with a smash hit, Salaam Bombay. She was also the director of Kama Sutra: A Tale of Love. She was born and raised in Delhi and educated at Delhi University and Harvard University. Since 1947, Delhi has become primarily a Punjabi populated city. She realized that the best celebration to depict would be the most extravagant, blissful, and most family-oriented: a Punjabi wedding. This film was shot by Declan Quinn on digital video. He captures the most intimate and gorgeous shots of the city life and cityscape, illuminating the actors on screen and creating beautiful thematic shots of flowers, lights, and an abundance of beautiful colors. The screenplay was written by Sabrina Dhawan, who grew up in New Delhi, India and studied in the graduate film program at Columbia University.

The large cast was extremely talented and exuded a great chemistry together on screen. The cast included one of India’s greatest actors, Naseeruddin Shah, who played the father of the bride. He portrays the caring father figure with great presence, especially in the last few scenes, in which he is driven to make an important decision for the sake of his family. Shah has acted in over 120 movies, winning numerous awards. Other notable actors include Lillete Dubey who plays the mother of the bride is a respected stage actress and theater director. Shefali Shetty who plays the cousin of the bride, Ria is applauded for her role in “Satya,” which won her an Indian Screen Award. She delivers an excellent performance as the bride’s cousin, who has to come to terms with a secret she has kept from her family for years. The only disappointment of the film was the lack of development of the lead character, the bride, Vasundhara Das, who is trying to resolve an old love affair days before her wedding. Indeed it wasn’t really Nair’s intention to focus on the bride and groom, but on the dimensions of the family, which she is successful in doing.

Monsoon wedding is a film about the celebration of marriage, family, and love. The influence of globalism and evidence of diaspora in India are greatly seen in this film. This film unites the traditional and modern India. Nair tests the strength of this family and they are given a beautiful conclusion. Nair says, “Monsoon Wedding freely invokes Bollywood conventions, musically and visually. This is a Bollywood movie, made on my own terms. If the film captures the mast- the intoxicating zest for life- of my people, then I will have done my work.” Monsoon Wedding does not only this but also invokes a zest for life and the union of family in its audiences as well.

20th San Francisco International Asian American Film Festival:A Preview

charlotte lin and roger wong

 

What started with 13 films in 1982 has exploded into 135 films and 43 programs featured between March 7 and March 17 at AMC Kabuki 8 Theaters in San Francisco, Pacific Film Archive in Berkeley, and Camera 3 Cinemas in San Jose. The festival includes experimental, independent, documentary, feature and short films that explore Asian American cinema’s past, present, and future. Organized by the National Asian American Telecommunications Association (NAATA), the festival showcases films by and about the Asian diaspora from all over the world including the United States, Canada, Taiwan, China, Japan, Philippines, Australia, France, India, South Korea, Pakistan, Thailand, Hong Kong, United Kingdom, and Singapore.

NAATA has been funding, producing, distributing, exhibiting films, videos, and media to support Asian Pacific American artists as well as promote and raise awareness of the Asian Pacific American communities. Among the twenty world premieres is Romeo Candido’s director debut, Lolo’s Child. Justin Lin’s Better Luck Tomorrow will premier in San Francisco on opening night while Gail Dogin’s award winning documentary Daughter from Danang and Timonthy Bui’s Green Dragon will be shown on the final night of the festival.

Better Luck Tomorrow

Ben Manibag (Parry Shen), a high school overachiever with a tunnel vision to Stanford, slowly finds himself tumbling into a life of mischief and corruption. Under the pressures of the competitive world of valedictorians, he innocently begins with cheat sheets and shoplifting and later finds himself leading a double life with guns, drugs, and Las Vegas escapades. This film is both a dark parody of the cutthroat college application process and an exploration of suburban teen restlessness in Asian America.

This long anticipated follow-up to Shopping For Fangs (1997) by Justin Lin (writer, editor, director, producer), Better Luck Tomorrow premiered at the Sundance Film Festival and was well received. Lin wanted to explore his own life growing up as an Asian American in SoCal suburbs during the 80s and today’s upper middle class youth’s urban gangsta mentality. Lin has directed numerous award-winning shorts, winning the UCLA’s Spotlight award and Honorable Mention at the Chicago Asian-American Film Festival for his Soybean Milk along the way.

Better Luck Tomorrow will be screened on opening night at 7PM , March 7, at AMC Kabuki Theaters, followed by an opening night gala reception with Director Lin at Fort Mason Center Landmark Building A, San Francisco.

Green Dragon

The festival will close with a film that visits the Vietnamese refugee processing camp, San Diego’s Camp Pendleton, near the end of the Vietnam War. From director and writer Timothy Linh Bui, the story focuses on a few refugees as they befriend each other and learn to depend on one another. The film follows the journey of a young Vietnamese boy, Pham (Trung Hieu Nguyen), and his uncle turned camp manager Tai Tran (Don Duong), and a volunteer cook, Addie (Forest Whitaker). More than a poignant look into the lives of Vietnamese refugees as they flee the fall of Saigon, the film unravels their paths to recovery from the horrors of war and the discovery of new meaning in life.

Green Dragon will be screened 7PM, March 14, at the AMC Kabuki Theaters followed by a closing night gala reception with Director Bui at the Diego Rivera Gallery, San Francisco Art Institution, 800 Chestnut Street, San Francisco.

Daughter from Danang

Separated from her mother at the end of Vietnam war, this documentary follows the life of a woman as she reunites with her mother after twenty-two years. Mai Thi Hiep was among thousands of orphans and Amerasian children, whose parents feared for their safeties, brought to the United States to be adopted through “Operation Babylift.” Renamed Heidi Neville and raised in Tennessee, she goes on a search to find her biological mother, Mai Thi Kim, in Vietnam. Culture clash and conflicting expectations soon follow a happy reunion as Heidi feels alienated from her lost family yet pressured to help them financially. Directors Gail Dolgin and Vicente Franco follow the two women through the emotional search, poignant reunion, and heartbreaking departure. This film is the winner of the Grand Jury Prize for Documentary at the 2002 Sundance Film Festival.

Daughter From Danang will be screened 2:45PM, March 16, at the Camera 3 Cinemas, Second and San Carlos Streets, San Jose; and 7:30PM, March 13 at the Pacific Film Archive with an appearance of filmmakers Dolgin and Franco.

Lolo's Child

Making its world premiere at the film festival, director/writer/editor/actor Romeo Candido brings a story of a songwriter who learns the history of his family, friends, and roots as he comes to terms with his father’s death. Candido’s debut feature, described as “the first Pilipino Canadian musical dramedy”, is an Asian family melodrama, comedy with a serious undertone, and a lyrical story incorporating Candido’s songs.

Lolo’s Child will premiere 9:30PM, March 8, and 9:15, March 11, AMC Kabuki Theaters.

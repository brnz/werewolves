South Asian DJs Throw it Down in the Bay Area
tour benefits victims of backlash

anmol chaddha

 

South Asian music artists across the diaspora are combining their art with activism by taking part in the movement for social justice around the world. While much of the desi art/activism has emerged from the UK, such as the politically conscious Asian Dub Foundation, the Bay Area took its place at the forefront of the movement with the kick-off of the Ahimsa Music Tour in San Francisco last month. The event represented both a response to hate crimes against Arabs, Muslims, and South Asians as well as the rise of the ‘Asian underground’ scene in the Bay Area.

Taking its name from the Jain ethic of non-violence, the Ahimsa Tour was organized to raise money for victims of the backlash against Arabs, Muslims, and South Asians since September 11th.

As Ahimsa organizer Vijay Chattha explained, “Right after the World Trade Center and Pentagon attacks, it was obvious that South Asians were going to be harassed...As more deaths and crimes occurred, we wanted to unite across religions, countries, and regions and see what we could do to help all of the victims.” According to Nihal Mehta, a Bay Area DJ and organizer of the Ahimsa Tour, the events seek to both “raise money and raise awareness” of the rising backlash against Arabs, Muslims, and South Asians.

Perhaps it is appropriate then that, along with uniting people across religious and ethnic lines, the artists on the Ahimsa Tour are also combining different musical styles in their productions. What has been termed ‘Asian underground’ emerged in England, where ‘Asian’ refers to what we label ‘South Asian’, and rose to popularity in the late-1990s. Often described with cliché phrases (such as ‘East Meets West’...or is it ‘West Eats Meat’?) and overused expressions, the music generally blends elements of ‘traditional’ South Asian music, such as tabla beats and sarangi melodies, with the ‘Western’ styles of hip-hop, drum and bass, dub, and house music. In many ways, the sounds reflect the cultural identities of the artists who are combining the ‘Western’ sounds they were raised on with the ‘traditional’ sounds of their ‘homeland.’

One of the first major figures of the ‘Asian underground’ — once it went above ground — was Talvin Singh from the UK, whose rock star status has earned him the disparaging nickname Talvin Klein. More recently, Karsh Kale from New York has enjoyed increased airplay while heightening the popularity of the ‘Asian underground’ in the US.

Heading up the Ahimsa Tour, however, are Rhino_FX (aka Musa Ahmed), Maneesh the Twister and Janaka, DJs from the Bay Area crew Dhamaal. According to the DJs, Dhamaal emerged when Azaad, the West Coast’s original ‘Asian underground’ collective founded in 1999, went into a voluntary hiatus this year.

Janaka explains that Azaad itself “started off as a random house party at [his] old place in Berkeley.” After a party was broken up at 1am, they came up with the idea of developing Azaad as a club event in San Francisco. Ahmed adds that they tried to create a space that “was not a traditional Bollywood/bhangra party nor a standard San Francisco club night.”

While the meaning of ‘dhamaal’ is well-debated, it loosely translates to “a throw down,” and Dhamaal events go far beyond the music. The crew is a collective of musicians and visual artists in which the DJs are only one part of the entire show. As a visually stimulating, interactive, multimedia experience, the shows also include live tabla players which produces “a full-on assault on nearly every sense,” according to Janaka. While the DJ summarizes Dhamaal events as “the improvisational blending of electronic, live acoustic, and live mixed visual images,” he insists that they are more than “the sum of the parts.”

Another important aspect of the Dhamaal crew is that its shows are organized as benefit events for worthy causes. In connecting the unique performance style with activism, Maneesh sees Dhamaal as being “a part of something that could give back to society and create something innovative at the same time.” In a sense, this reinforces the connection that the Dhamaal musicians make between the community and its cultural productions. With South Asian artists as the leading producers of these innovative sounds, Azaad co-founder Ahmed avoids “exoticizing [his] sound for the benefit of white folks.” Instead, the DJs use their skills to give back to their communities.

This outlook brought Rhino_FX, Maneesh the Twister and Janaka into the Ahimsa Music Tour last month. Specifically, the tour is raising money to develop a memorial to Balbir Singh Sodi, a Sikh gas station owner murdered in Mesa, Arizona; to assist the family of Waquar Hasan, a slain grocery store owner from Dallas, Texas; to help reconstruct the Hindu Temple that was burned down in Hamilton, Ontario; and to raise money to launch the Smithsonian Sikh Heritage Project in Washington, DC

The first show in San Francisco raised over $2500 and the tour is expected to raise over $50,000 as it continues through Los Angeles, New York City, Philadelphia, Miami, New Orleans, and Chicago in the next few months. From its roots in a Berkeley house party, the Dhamaal crew demonstrates the ways in which all aspects of our lives can be integrated into a larger vision of social justice.

For more information see the Ahimsa Music Tour: http://www.urbangroove.com/ahimsa; Dhamaal: http://www.azaadsf.com

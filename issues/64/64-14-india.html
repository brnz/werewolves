Out of India

by krishneil maharaj

Ever wondered about the future of the nationalist project? Consider this newspaper for instance. Under the broad umbrella of the identifier API, we try to cover a wide array of issues that affect the communities that are understood to be included in that category. In effect, hardboiled serves as a catalyst for bringing the progressive community together. To that end, my contribution to this paper has been to contribute the perspectives of South Asians and Pacific Islanders. But I would like to draw this into a much larger debate on the ramifications of ethnic nationalism and the future of progressivism on this campus.

Though I have been away from Cal for a while now, I still keep tabs on what’s going on. A particularly helpful medium is the Indus Forum email list. With my subscription, I am able to keep up with the latest developments: upcoming bhangra events, hindi films, parties, and the occasional banter inspired by some well-meaning but misguided individual. The list is also a great place to look for an MCB or EECS textbook. A few weeks ago, I got a flurry of emails as quite a few students got in on a discussion of Indian history. For a brief while, I was praying to receive a party email from wannabegangstah productions.

This exchange highlights a central concern for those of us struggling for progressive multi-culturalism in this nation. Perhaps as a byproduct of how immigrants construct their identities over generations and also out of the practicality of staying relevant to issues in the US, and not the politics of the homeland, there is not much discussion of how ethnic-nationalism factors into the construction of progressivism.

It is something we should consider—especially those who still have particularly close ties abroad.

Take the case of a discussion on Indus mailing list—not that I want to slander Indus with the charge of being progressive. A student disgruntled by Professor Robert Goldman’s South Asian Studies course emailed the list with his displeasure. It seems what was bothering him was the way in which Professor Goldman had dismissed one theory on the settlement of ancient India for another.

At the core of the students’ argument is the belief that Goldman is an agent of the colonial venture by favoring the Aryan Invasion Theory over the Out of India Theory—helping in the colonizing of the knowledge of India.

What are these theories? They are theories on the settlement of ancient India—when, where, and the most important, how. They are two very different understandings of the trajectory of Indian history.

The Aryan Invasion Theory first appeared in the discourse of the British. It was employed to construct India’s identity as a wounded civilization. Glorious and mighty in its ancient era, it has been on a gradual decline since that ancient hey-day, as invaders chipped away at the splendor of vedic India. Since this is an Orientalist British history, then it has to be pointed out that this decline that India was in could only be remedied by the British colonial endeavor—the task of returning India to its ancient glory.

The 19th century was when it can be said that nationalist discourse took roots in many colonized societies—organized responses to foreign power, but with people mobilized by the rhetoric of very modern, post-enlightenment ideas of nationalism. What this meant in India was that there was an Indian response to the British presence was a national one, and not one based on other identities. Just as in the Philippines, reaction was not based on a local or communal level, but national. The point is fine, but one not worth missing.

When India became independent, its new leaders did not challenge the British understanding of history, instead they chose to insert themselves into the narrative—stipulating that the Indian state now possessed the British mantle of returning India to its ancient greatness.
The anti-colonial environment of the 19th and early 20th centuries brought forth many ideas. Some of the most popular were the backlash against the perceived indignities offered by the British theories on the origins of the Indian people. So, homegrown rhetoric began to counter the production of this knowledge. Out of that milieu was produced the Out of India Theory. Running counter to the Aryan Invasion Theory, it states that the motley tribes known as the Aryans actually originated in India and settled in a northwestern direction from India to Europe. There is much more evidence to support the opposite position—the migration of various tribes from the Caucasus region into South Asia.

This issue is boring. Playing Dungeons and Dragons is more exciting than talking about these theories. There literally is a million things in Indian history more deserving of study. But, it remains at the forefront of discussion because the Out of India Theory is has been husbanded by Hindu nationalists and their small band of pseudo-scholars like David Frawley, Pierre Gautier, and Subhash Kak. These individuals are celebrated for their unfortunate fascination with outdated theories of race, religion, history, etc. The first two are disaffected westerners who seek to offer the validation of Hindu nationalism, that only disaffected westerners can give (be VERY scared of Islam!). The third individual is an engineering professor from a Louisiana university who has made the unfortunate mistake of extending his academic credentials to an area where he has no understanding. Still, they are extremely popular in the South Asian American community because their ideologies offer a salve for a group that has the task of crafting its identity outside of its national territory.
Remember, what is important here is the impact these conservative ideologies of the homeland have on the development of progressive ideas here in the US. We only have to take a look at Zionism and how overseas Jews, especially those residing in the US are some of the most ardent supporters. What this means for those of us with international connections is that we have to make sure we have an in-depth understanding of the history—as well as the debate around the history.

I have been told that if you walk around an Indian city today, it is possible to pick up a Nazi swastika from street vendors. Not the Hindu swastika, but the Nazi one. The popularity of these fascist ideologies in India is very concerning. The ideological connection between 1930s German nationalism and Hindu nationalism is uncanny—down to the fascination with martial authority and fashion.

Those of us with family or other connections abroad must be wary of the histories and ideologies that are presented to us. The anti-colonial struggles from places as diverse as Indonesia, India, and the Philippines provide the stories that can be used to inspire progressive action here. Ethnic chauvinism and ethno-centrism is subtle, but it has to be rooted out because it will get us nowhere.

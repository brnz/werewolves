Seeking: Pilipino Amercian Professor for intellectual discourse...

marites mendoza

 

UC Berkeley’s Asian American Studies department is arguably one of the country’s most well known programs of its kind. Its experts are frequently consulted for their take on Asian American issues, while students and scholars gather at their feet in hopes of partaking in their wisdom. As high profile as the faculty and department are, however, their inadequacies in providing for the Pilipino American community — the second largest Asian American group in the nation — bring shame to not only the department, but the University as well.

According to the 2000 census results, Pilipino Americans make up 18% of the country’s Asian American population, behind only Chinese Americans. A good portion resides right here in California, especially the Bay Area. And yet one would never know of the community’s significance by looking at Berkeley’s Asian American Studies faculty. In terms of representation, Japanese, Chinese, Korean, and even the burgeoning Southeast Asian American community have faces and experts to look up to in the department. Pilipino students on the other hand, see no faces like their own in any department but Nutritional Science.

The need for a ladder-rank Pilipino American professor goes beyond mere numbers and representation. What happens to Pilipino Americans can be studied in relation to other ethnic minorities. “The relationship between Pilipinos and America is like a litmus test,” said Raissa Diamente, a core member of the student group Committee for Pilipino American Studies (ComPASs). “It’s a test case for history.” Indeed, the Philippines has one of the most long and complex histories of Western colonization. The effects of imperialism persist today, as evidenced in Pilipino culture and traditions. Closer to campus, Pilipino Americans were the first ethnic group to be dropped from Affirmative Action in 1989. According to a Statement of Concern written in 1996 by Pilipino Academic Student Services, this move resulted in a 36.7% drop in Pilipino admits from the applicant pool from 1989-1996, the biggest drop of all ethnic minority groups during that period. Once again, Pilipino Americans served as the “litmus test” for the effects of the removal of Affirmative Action.

With at least 700 Pilipino students and seven highly active Pilipino organizations on campus, the time has come to provide a Pilipino ladder-rank faculty member who can study and teach students about the Pilipino experience. To the Ethnic Studies department’s credit, they’ve been trying for the past ten years to secure the money for the position, placing a request for funding in their budget each year. Each time, however, their attempts have been thwarted by either the University, which must approve each allotment for new faculty, or because of issues internal to the department itself. Timing has also been one of the main obstacles to the search. “It has taken time to work out which programs would be allotted FTE [Full Time Equivalent]. Both Chicano and Native American Studies experienced more retirements and more people quitting than Asian American Studies,” said Elaine Kim, a professor in Asian American Studies and a big supporter of the effort. In addition to Asian American Studies faculty, Pilipino American students have also done their part by continuously urging the campus administration and Ethnic Studies to act.

Considering their numbers, Pilipino students have proved to be one of the most active Asian American groups on campus. When Amado Cabezas was denied tenure in the Department of Ethnic Studies in 1992, undergraduate and graduate students formed an ad hoc committee to investigate the case and launched a campaign for redress. In just one semester, the group was able to gather support and attention in the forms of 3,000 letters written to Chancellor Chang-Lin Tien, a demonstration in front California Hall, and meetings with the Chancellor and then-Provost Carol Christ. Their efforts, however, were not enough to secure Cabezas’ position. This year when Ethnic Studies takes another stab at requesting funding, they will again be backed by a group of well-organized and determined students. ComPASs was formed last spring as a way to organize students and allow continuity in the fight to establish Pilipino American Studies. They’ve played an important role in conducting research on Pilipino American Studies, gathering information about the urgency of bringing to Berkeley an expert in the area. One of the committee’s short terms goals is to organize a conference focusing on Pilipino American Studies with the hopes of attracting more attention from the University and academia in general. The road they’ve traveled since last Spring has been a rough one, including a mix of successful and failed attempts to gather support from within the University and Ethnic Studies. The students have already had to sustain one let-down this past summer when they were told that although the funding had been attained for the position last Spring, the search for a professor would not begin this Fall as planned.

And so the cycle will begin again. ComPASs has already secured the definite support of the Ethnic Studies Department and its chairman, Jose Saldivar, and Letters and Sciences Dean George Breslauer, both of whom have said the effort is their “highest priority” according to Dexter Ligot-Gordon of ComPASs. The final step is the approval by the University’s Budget Committee of the allotment for the position. This may prove to be the toughest gate to pass through, however, as has been the case for several years in the past. “I think the biggest challenge will be to find someone whose scholarship and qualifications will be recognized by the Budget Committee,” said Professor Kim, who believes that this will be the year the request will be granted.

Unfortunately, the Budget Committee won’t decide on the issue until late Spring 2002. Even if they approve the funding, the search for a professor won’t start until Summer 2002, which means that a professor won’t be in place until Fall 2003, according to ComPASs projections. In the meantime, ComPASs and its core, composed primarily of juniors and seniors, will continue its efforts. “We’re working for something we’ll never see,” said Ligot-Gordon. Still, it is important work that will benefit all of the future students at Cal, both Pilipino Americans or otherwise.

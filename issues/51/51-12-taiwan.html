Taiwan for Taiwanese

andy s. chang

 

On September 8, a group of Taiwanese Americans gathered for a rally in San Francisco to commemorate an accord that was signed between the United States, Japan, and 46 other nations half a century ago—the San Francisco Peace Treaty. In this treaty, Japan formally surrendered “all right, title and claims to Formosa and the Pescadores,” but did not designate any beneficiary state nor restore Taiwan to Chinese rule. The status of the former Japanese colony was left unresolved long after the end of the Second World War. The Formosan Association for Public Affairs, the premier Taiwanese American interest group, states that the pact formed the “basis for Taiwan’s self-determination [and] independence.”

According to Taiwan’s Central News Agency, more than 500 Taiwanese Americans attended the rally at San Francisco’s UN Plaza. U.S. Rep. Nancy Pelosi (D-Calif.), a guest speaker at the plaza, said Taiwan is a “flourishing democratic country” and urged the international community to allow the nation to participate in international affairs.

Following Pelosi’s speech, the demonstrators marched toward Union Square, while displaying signs and shouting, “Taiwan belongs to the Taiwanese,” to make the public aware that Taiwan’s fate is to be decided by the island’s own residents; the marchers received warm support from passersby and car drivers on Market Street.  

A symposium at the Grand Hyatt concluded the 50th anniversary celebrations in San Francisco. Lung-chu Chen, a professor at New York Law School; Richard Kagan, professor of history at Hamline University; and Tom Campbell, professor of law at Stanford University and former U.S. Rep. (R-Calif.), engaged in discussions on the treaty’s significance to Taiwan’s sovereignty. One of the topics discussed at the symposium was America’s stake in the Taiwan question—Kagan said the peace treaty with Japan “did not establish the peace. And because of this, the war in Asia is still simmering. But this time, the major antagonists are not the U.S. and Japan, but Beijing and Taipei.”

Since the San Francisco Peace Treaty is the only legal document that exists pertaining to Taiwan’s status, self-determination by Taiwanese citizens is the solution that best reflects the principles of democracy and the Charter of the United Nations. But such an option requires world support and, especially, the commitment of Taiwan’s strongest ally—America—in condemning China’s tactics of intimidation toward the Taiwanese.

At the closing ceremony of the symposium, Professor Campbell cited the Gulf War as an example of the need for clarity in the American foreign policy. On the eve of Iraq’s invasion of Kuwait, the U.S. made it clear that it would not engage in warfare, which probably gave Iraq felicitous signals to attack Kuwait immediately afterward. “The cause of world peace, as well as basic political freedom, is best advanced by America stating clearly that it will defend its ally, Taiwan, if it is attacked” were Campbell’s final words in his speech, and words we should not forget.

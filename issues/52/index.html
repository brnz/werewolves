editor's note

To our biggest fans:

Thanks for your enthusiastic reception of hb 5.1. We ran out of issues after the first week and a half of distribution. Hopefully this unprecedented historical phenomenon is a sign that API activism is growing at a time when there is little support for critical, oppositional voices in the media.

Now many of you have asked what hardboiled is and what it means (we seem to revisit this question every year), so here’s the answer for the 5 series. Yes, the Founders of hardboiled, the APIA student publication here at Cal, did name it in some sort of reference to that John Woo flick. The term “hardboiled” usually refers to a detective/cop that’s been burned and has become devoid of sentimentality. Former hb editor David K. Song considers the term a way for Asian Americans to assert their American-ness, for the term refers to classic American dime novels (refer to ed note of 3.1). I’ve always thought hardboiled was a funny way of displaying masculinity, since it refers to an egg, a signifier of femininity. But regardless of what hardboiled means, just don’t confuse us with that other eggy publication,Yolk, now that we’ve finally gotten rid of the Asian Avenue ad (hopefully we didn’t lose too much readership over that). An egg, in identity politics of color, being white on the outside and yellow on the inside, is an inverse of a banana or a twinkie. But I’m not exactly into cultural essentialism and pointing fingers as to who is white-washed and who is not.

Instead of talking about yellow-white relations, I would like to address the yellow-brown relationship within the APIA community in light of the recent events. In all our Asian American studies classes, the case of Vincent Chin has always exemplified why we APIAs form coalitions; hate crimes is an issue of racial lumping and affects all members of the APIA community. However, the recent hate crime attacks on one segment of the APIA community has not seemed to get much response from the community as a whole. Shortly after S11, I went to a forum on Asians in the Greek system. Many of the participants, composed of mainly Chinese and Korean Americans, did not feel that racial hate crimes could happen at Cal. This forum took place one day before another forum addressed the recent hate crimes (some on campus) against South Asian, Central Asian, and Southwest Asian Americans. While in some communities, hate crimes and racial profiling are the biggest issues of current concern, others are more concerned about elimination of the SAT I, and often these different interests split along colorlines. Although some of us are feel that yellow and brown were artificially put into the same category as “Asian” simply because the immense size of Asia, many issues still bind us together: Orientalism, the Model-Minority Myth, and being perceived as foreigners or politically apathetic engineers or doctors.

Overall, like many Asian American Studies departments across the country, hb has been too yellow-centric in the past and we are trying hard to increase our brown representation.

Finally, for those of you angry at our publication, please adhere to the following hb etiquettes when expressing your anger:

Do not yell at the tall South Asian guy at Sather Gate just because you dislike a specific article -- that’s what letters to the editors are for.

Do not punch the front cover of the newspaper -- it looks bad when you’re a white guy punching a representation of a Sikh face.

If you feel that hb is not “objective” enough or too “one-sided,” first of all, be wary of anyone that claims to be objective and realize that a centrist voice does not necessarily equate an objective voice (if such a thing actually exists). Secondly, do not start a glossy publication that claims to be “non-partisan” with a huge American Flag on the cover and drain our prop 2 funds when the “non-partisan” view is readily available via the Associate Press, New York Times, CNN, MSNBC, ABC, and whichever other news station that has received anthrax threats.

And if you’re still angry about the Asian fetish article in issue 3.6, realize that we’re not neo-nazis and we did not say that white men should only date white women. And if you’re still angry after this realization: I have just one recommendation for our Orientalist friends: Hookahs not bazookas!

Peace,
Yueni Zhong

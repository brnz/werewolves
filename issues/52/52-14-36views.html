Taking a Look at 36 Views

jack song

 

The play 36 Views written by Naomi Iizuka, is a story full of deceit and self-deception. It challenges the audience’s expectations and perception of reality and fantasy.

Darius Wheeler, played by Bill Camp, opens the play with his wild tales of his travel in Southeast Asia. This avid Oriental art collector’s exaggerated gestures and tone accentuate his machismo and presumptuous attitude toward his love interest, Setsujo Hearn, an art scholar. In the background, there are the aspiring con-artists Claire Tsung, who creates ancient artwork and sells these “antiques” to high bidders, and John Bell, who works for Wheeler and tricks him into buying Tsung’s artwork, the so-called Pillow Book. This fake Pillow Book had supposedly changed the history of Asian antiquity. The ending, however, surprises the audience when the twist and turns gradually come together to form the bigger picture.

Rather than being intellectually enlightening, the play becomes superficial with a heavy emphasis on the sex scenes and Hearn’s appearance.

The most interesting element of 36 Views is the interaction between the audience and the play itself. Director Mark Wing-Davey does a great job placing the characters closely to the audience in order to involve the latter in the story as well. Characters such as Wheeler and Tsung often look directly to the crowd as they are challenging the audience’s perception of reality and fantasy.

The set also captures the audience attention scene after scene. Strong visual contrasts such as traditional Japanese art versus contemporary art and constantly moving props creates great entertainment. Set designer Douglass Stein uses the sliding screens and floating panels to subtly create different worlds for the characters. Also, the computer-generated projection captures the convergence of eastern and western cultures, with the help of heavy Japanese rap in the background.

While the technical aspect of 36 Views is eye-catching, it is its theme that truly forces the audience to think critically and doubt their perception. Iizuka tries to present the importance of dispensing our expectations and viewing life from a different perspective. She challenges society’s view toward the Asian American woman as exotic and vulnerable. Setsujo Hearn portrays that stereotypical image: sexy, weak, and intelligent. However, she is the exact opposite of what the audience expects. She is not Japanese. She is born in China and raised in Ohio by her adoptive parents.

The message the play delivers is clear but weak. It tries to be philosophical, toying with western society’s pervasive image of Asian American women. Rather than being intellectually enlightening, the play becomes superficial with a heavy emphasis on the sex scenes and Hearn’s appearance.

Filled with art-speak, jargon and pillow-book poetry, Naomi Iizuka does succeed in creating a visually entertaining play. In order to understand what the playwright tries to present, the audience must not go to the theater as critics, but rather as actors in the play.

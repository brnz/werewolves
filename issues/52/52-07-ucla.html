UCLA Students Demand South Asian Studies

anmol chaddha

 

In the aftermath of the September 11 attacks, the US has turned its eyes and bombs towards Afghanistan, while ‘patriots’ at home have curiously taken out their frustrations on Sikhs, whose turbans linked them to Osama bin Laden and made them distinct victims of the backlash. Much has since been said about how Sikhism actually originated in Northern India, has no connection to Afghanistan, and is a religion entirely distinct from Islam. Nonetheless, this confusion reflects a collective ignorance about South Asia, and has further energized UCLA students in their struggle for a South Asian studies program on their campus.

What does it take for a geographic region to warrant an academic program at a university? Is it enough that one out of every six people in the world lives there? These are the questions asked by the students who, concerned about the lack of South Asian classes offered at the university, established the South Asian Studies Task Force in the Winter Quarter 2001.

Having petitioned university administrators with their demands, the students decry the lack of consistently offered courses and explain in the mission statement of the organization that “the extent of courses offered does not do justice to the wealth of potential studies in the region of South Asia.”

In addition to the approximately 1,000 South Asian students at UCLA, the growing presence of over 200,000 South Asians in the Los Angeles area emphasizes the absurdity of the current situation at UCLA in which there exists no undergraduate minor, major, or graduate program of study for the subcontinent.

By demanding a relevant education, South Asian students are resisting the marginalization of South Asians in all academic disciplines. Students add that even the Asian American studies curriculum tends to focus on East Asian Americans — a critique that has often been waged against the Asian American studies program at UC-Berkeley as well.

Specifically, the task force is urging the university to grant tenure and hire more South Asian studies professors. As the students explain, “an investment in South Asian studies is an investment to the commitment to diversity and research at UCLA.” The university’s response to the demands has been characterized as “ambiguous” with no tangible commitment to the program.

Reminiscent of the campaign to defend Ethnic Studies at UC-Berkeley in Spring 1999, the students agitating for the creation of South Asian studies at UCLA understand that the issues go beyond the creation and maintenance of one program. Instead, they place their struggle in the larger context of exercising student voice and demanding institutional accountability in the educational process.

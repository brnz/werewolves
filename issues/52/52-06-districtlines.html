New Lines, Same Lies
redrawing of district lines dilute APA power

jinny ahn

 

The 2000 Census verified that San Francisco’s 12th State Assembly District has the most Asian Americans in California. But whether this will give Asian Americans any more political power is debatable as new district lines are being drawn in the Bay Area.

Currently, the state legislature is reallocating California’s 33 million person population into 80 assembly districts based on the new census. Because California’s overall population has grown at a higher rate than SF’s, the city’s two districts have to add populations from beyond its limits-- thus creating the possibility for a district with the highest Asian American population in the state. However, with the drawing of new district lines, this was far from true. The lines dilute Asian American political power by splitting the APA community into two different districts. Ideally, the Asian community would be kept together in one district, thereby increasing their legitimacy and their influence in the state legislature. Issues like immigration rights, affirmative action and bilingual education are not strictly Asian American issues, but with a majority ethnic population in a district, the person representing that district would better serve these interests. Redrawing district lines forms the political landscape for the next 10 years, which is why it is so important that it reflects the communities it encompasses.

The recent redrawing of district lines in California has had a political impact on not only Asian Americans, but also gays in the Castro district and Latinos in Southern California, who have all been again marginalized by the redrawing of district lines that cuts across their communities.

Reapportionment, the re-drawing of legislative district lines occurs, every ten years after the census reveals changes in populations, and has historically been manipulated by legislators and those in power to divide and conquer minority populations. In order to protect the current political establishment of San Francisco and Santa Clara County Asian populations were divided up so that their impact would be lessened.

San Francisco, which has an Asian population of 31 percent, is divided between two newly redrawn districts -- the 12th and 13th district. The 12th district reaches into the western half and three-quarters of the southern parts of San Francisco, the heavily Asian American neighborhoods such as the Richmond, Sunset, Excelsior and Visitacion Valley. The number of Asians in the 12th district is increased significantly by the inclusion of half of Daly City, which itself is 50 percent Asian. The 13th Assembly District, which takes in the western half of SF and half of Daly City is 42 percent Asian. By dividing SF and Daly City, the new districts weaken the influence of Asian voters in San Francisco.

At the same time, the Daly City residents of the 12th District are practically disenfranchised politically because whoever will represent the district will most likely come from SF and be more concerned with SF issues. Thus, for the considerable APA population in Daly City, they won’t have the political influence they deserve.

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 plays an important role in the apportionment of representation. The Voting Rights Act guarantees one person one vote and maximizes equal representation. It protects against the dilution of minority votes and protects “communities of interests.” Although not required by law to create a district in which a recognized minority or combination of minorities is in the majority, lawmakers must give consideration to the concentration of minority populations when drawing the lines.

Each jurisdiction will be required to prove to the U.S. Department of Justice that the proposed reapportionment plan does not worsen the positions of minority voters in comparison to that political subdivision’s most recently-approved districting plan. For example, reapportionment plans are prohibited from dividing up a geographically situated minority population into different districts in order to minimize their voting strength in any one district. Conversely, it is also illegal to draw a majority of a county’s minority population into a single district in order to minimize their opportunity to hold multiple seats on the governing board.

Not only would the ideal district map not reduce the voting strength of any minority or combination of minorities, but it would facilitate the election of minority representation where the opportunity for such facilitation exists. But in light of what has recently happened in SF, that is not likely to occur any time soon. Reapportionment, when still used as a tool for the few and elite to maintain the status quo, has long term consequences for minority communities to get their issues heard and advocated at the legislative level.

The census figures are used for the reapportionment and redistricting process that determines representation in Washington. These figures are also used to determine whether that representation is adequate -- if the representation is reflective of the diversity of people who live in that state and in that newly cut congressional district. Its important to make sure that the districts are cut in a way that’s consistent with the people who live there. The 2000 Census puts SF’s population at 776,000 and San Mateo County’s is 707,000. San Mateo County currently has 43.5 % Asian American, while non-Latino whites went from 49.8% of the district to 36.3%. Each of the state’s 80 Assembly districts should have about 423,000 people. Yet, SF ended up as the dominant portion of two Assembly districts, at the expense of surrounding counties.

Census statistics are used for the allocation of programmatic dollars -- everything from education and health care to transportation, which is why there was such a push by community organizations to get an accurate Census report. In 1990, dozens of communities, ethnic minorities and lower income communities, lost out on millions of federal funding due to inaccurate counting by the Census bureau. But now, after the 2000 Census, it looks as if some of these same communities will lose out again, this time in the dilution of their political voice in state government.

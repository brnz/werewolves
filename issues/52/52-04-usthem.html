Us and Them
Divisive Approach to Terrorism Threatens Human Rights Agenda

dharushana muthulingam

 

The bombers have rolled out, American troops have mobilized, and British troops are already there. We’ve set out nobly and self righteously, food packages in one arm, and an M3A1 Carbine grenade launcher under the other. George W. Bush calls has named this war “our calling.” The world has moved en masse to fight a “war on terrorism,” but this is a strangely suspect term. In the post cold war, have we yet again polarized the people of the world into “us and them,” looking once more for a common cause, an enemy to unite against?

But at what point is it a united community, and at what point is it one mob pitted against another?

Professor. John M. Post of George Washington University Medical School, in a paper prepared for an international conference on the Psychological Foundations of Terrorism, analyzes the language of radical terrorists: “’They’, the establishment, are the source of all evil in vivid contrast to ‘us,’ the freedom fighters, consumed by righteous rage.’”

Americans have noted this first hand, as fundamentalists decry the establishments of capitalism and the exported culture. “These events have divided the world into two camps,” spoke Mr. Bin Laden, “ the camp of the faithful, and the camp of infidels.” It is a holy war, and the enemy must be destroyed. It is extremist, unreasonable, and shocking.

But does it just as easily work in reverse? New York City Mayor Giuliani, greatly lauded for his leadership during the crisis, nonetheless incites anxiety in his October 1 speech to the United Nations.

“This is not a time for further study or vague directives,” he declared. “There is no room for neutrality on the issue of terrorism. You’re either with civilization or with terrorists. On one side is democracy, the rule of law and respect for human life; on the other side is tyranny, arbitrary executions and mass murder. We’re right and they’re wrong. It’s as simple as that.”

Is this also extremist, unreasonable, shocking?

We establish the world is now “us” and “them,” but where is the line between the two? Who is civilization and who are the terrorist? What to say of a civilized government that carries out acts of terror? What are the sacrifices so called civilization is willing to make to defeat the “enemy”?

The US thus far, as any self perpetuating entity, has acted in its own interest. It decried human rights violations and yet sold land mines to the violators.

Note the case of Sri Lanka. The country has suffered violent ethnic conflict for the last twenty years. Minority ethnic Tamils in the Northeast had long claimed political disenfranchisement, discrimination in higher education, and a host of other grievances with the national government since independence. Twenty years ago, the struggle turned violent when the separatist group LTTE took over, and the country had been engaged in civil war since. Both the Sri Lankan government and the LTTE had been cited for human rights violations such arbitrary civilian death, torture, and information censorship. Government forces also have on their list mass graves, food as a weapon of war, rape as a weapon of war, police brutality, arbitrary detention, and various others. Despite the acts of terror committed by both sides, the Sri Lankan government is labeled as “civilization” by the US and the minority LTTE, as terrorist.

Labels may be arbitrary, but weapons of mass destruction are not. In 1996, the US , claiming “no dog in this fight,” as American military attache Col. Carl Kockrum put it, acted other wise, selling the government patrol boats, guns, and military helicopters, as well as deploying American Green Beret specialist to train Sri Lankan troupes. This occurred despite the official ban on selling US lethal weapons to a country with such a hefty human rights records. The State Department claimed a recent “dramatic” improvement in the country’s record, although human rights groups within the country claimed gross overstatement.

The Pentagon, usually publicizing such joint military exercises with great fanfare, was fairly covert in the matter, as was the Sri Lankan government. The relationship between US and Sri Lankan officials may have sprung from the Gulf War, in which Sri Lanka was the only Asian nation to allow the US to build refueling stations on their land. US officials also admit they are attracted to Sri Lanka’s strategic position between the Middle East and Far east and near China. It is a fairly common deal, in which a foreign military receives valuable training from the US, while US intelligence gains knowledge of the workings of the foreign military for potential later use. But in those other cases, those armies were not about to be engaged in an internal struggle with its own people. Many fear the Sri Lankan government will use the recent attacks and antiterrorist sentiment to perpetuate its human rights violations with more monetary support as than ever more.

A more recent example of the contradictions in Giuliani’s “civilization” is the case of the Cairo 52, in which police raided a gay club in Cairo, Egypt last May and arrested 52 men, detaining them for accusations ranging from “obscene behavior” to running a homosexual “cult” Egypt, an ally of the United States, is guilty of various human rights violations: torture under detention, lack of legal representation, names, pictures and addresses published in State-run media. Criminal penalties for consensual homosexual activity violates international law. Also, gross irregularities and inconsistencies in the case indicate the men may have been framed in order to appease conservative and religious forces.

Complicating the matter was the September 11 attack. The men had been tried in a court in which the ruling cannot be overturned, violating international codes for a fair trial. All 52 men were under accusations which amounted to “organizing to undermine the state,” an act that can be considered terrorism. Since the US attack, the lead defendant in the case has been accused of having connections to Osama bin Laden. “Egypt is going to use terrorism as an excuse to crack down on the local gay community,” said Faisal Alam, leader of a gay Muslim group watching the events.

Sharon Burke, of Amnesty International, is also dubious, claiming the charges constantly change so police can justify the arrests, since there is no specific antihomosexual law in Egypt. Also the “[terrorist] thing is suspect, given the timing,” she said.

The verdict is scheduled to pass November 12. Until then various organizations, as well as some US congressman, have been undertaking a letter writing campaign to Egyptian embassies. Many human rights group globally fear the US, in its haste to build a front against terrorism, will easily overlook blatant violations of rights committed by those on the side of “civilization.” For effective long-term relationships, such concerns are crucial. “We would urge the US. Government to keep human rights on the agenda,” she said. And perhaps not be so hasty in its divisive and hypocritical rhetoric.
